# Cognitive Profile: Paul Graham (Y Combinator)

**Source:** Essays (paulgraham.com), YC interviews, startup advice

**Domain:** Startup evaluation, product strategy, founder assessment

---

## Decision Framework

### 1. Strip to Core
**Question:** What problem does this ACTUALLY solve?

**Red flags:**
- Vague problem statement ("helps people be more productive")
- Solution in search of a problem
- Solving a problem that doesn't hurt anyone

**Green flags:**
- Specific, painful problem founder experienced personally
- Can explain problem in one sentence
- Problem exists whether or not solution exists

---

### 2. Founder Fit
**Question:** Can THIS team pull this off? Why them specifically?

**Red flags:**
- No domain expertise
- Never experienced the problem themselves
- "We'll hire someone who knows X"
- MBA/consultant background with no technical skills

**Green flags:**
- Founder experienced pain personally, built solution for self
- Technical founder who can build the product
- Obsessive about the problem (thinking about it for years)
- "Live in the future" - see what's coming before others

---

### 3. Market Timing
**Question:** Why now? What changed in the world?

**Red flags:**
- "Technology is ready now" (was ready 5 years ago too)
- No specific catalyst
- "People will eventually want this"

**Green flags:**
- Recent regulatory change
- New enabling technology (not "internet is big")
- Behavior shift (e.g., remote work after COVID)
- Can name the inflection point

---

### 4. Traction Pattern
**Question:** What does early growth look like?

**Red flags:**
- No users yet ("we're in stealth")
- Users don't love it (lukewarm "it's fine")
- Retention is terrible
- "We need marketing to get users"

**Green flags:**
- Organic growth (users telling other users)
- Founder knows all early users personally
- Weekly iteration based on feedback
- Users would be upset if product went away

---

### 5. Ambitious Vision + Narrow Start
**Question:** Do they start narrow but think big?

**Red flags:**
- "Everyone" is the target market
- Boil the ocean strategy (enterprise + consumer + B2B)
- No specific first customer identified

**Green flags:**
- Narrow wedge (specific use case, specific customer)
- Can name the first 10 customers
- Big vision is plausible extension of narrow start
- "Do things that don't scale" mindset

---

## Question Sequence (In Order)

1. **What do you do?** (one sentence)
2. **Who desperately needs this?** (specific person, not demographic)
3. **What do they do now?** (status quo)
4. **Why does that hurt?** (pain point)
5. **Why will they switch?** (10x better, not 10% better)
6. **Why you?** (unfair advantage)
7. **Why now?** (timing)
8. **What's the insight?** (what do you know that others don't?)

**If answers are vague or generic → probably not fundable.**

---

## Contrarian Beliefs (Signature Paul Graham Thinking)

- "Make something people want" > "build a great business"
- Hackers > MBAs
- Narrow focus > broad appeal (initially)
- Growth rate > absolute numbers
- Organic growth > marketing spend
- Fast iteration > planning
- Users loving you > users tolerating you

---

## What He Consistently Ignores

- Competition ("if you're not embarrassed by v1, you shipped too late")
- Business model (comes later, focus on growth first)
- Credentials (doesn't care about pedigree)
- Age (bias toward young founders but not absolute)

---

## Usage in Skills

When evaluating startup ideas, product strategy, or founder decisions:

```markdown
Apply Paul Graham (YC) framework:
1. Strip to core: What problem does this solve? (one sentence)
2. Founder fit: Why is THIS team uniquely positioned?
3. Market timing: Why now?
4. Traction pattern: Organic growth or forced?
5. Ambitious vision + narrow start?

If answers are vague → reject.
If answers are specific + founder experienced pain → green flag.
```
